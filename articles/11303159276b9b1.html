      Balwani and holmes

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

On December 7, 2022, Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani, the former lover and business partner of Theranos CEO Elizabeth Holmes, arrives in federal court in San Jose, California.

Former Theranos executive Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani was sentenced to nearly 13 years in prison by a federal judge in California for his role in a multimillion-dollar fraud involving Theranos and its now-disgraced CEO, Elizabeth Holmes, who received an 11-year sentence in a separate trial.

A jury found Balwani guilty on all 12 felony counts of defrauding Theranos investors and patients who used the company's untrustworthy blood tests in July.

According to sentencing memos sent to the court last month, U.S. District Judge Edward Davila's sentence for Balwani is less than federal prosecutors' demand for at least 15 years in prison, but far more than Balwani's attorney's request for a few months in prison.

In comparison, Holmes was convicted of four counts of wire fraud and sentenced to a little more than 11 years in prison last month.

His attorneys stated in court on Wednesday and in court documents that Balwani should be treated differently than Holmes. Balwani put nearly $5 million of his personal funds into Theranos and lost it all.




"Mr. Balwani is not the same as Elizabeth Holmes: he actually invested millions of dollars of his own money; he never sought fame or recognition; and he has a long history of quietly giving to those less fortunate (dating well before his time at Theranos) without seeking recognition or benefit," Balwani's attorneys wrote to the judge in a memo. "All of these characteristics define Mr. Balwani as a person." In court, his attorneys echoed this sentiment.

Balwani was Theranos' second-in-command and Holmes' former romantic partner, though Holmes claims the older Balwani sexually and emotionally abused her during their relationship. Balwani, through his attorney, has denied the allegations.

During their tenure as CEOs, the two claimed that Theranos offered never-before-seen technology that could scan patients for hundreds of illnesses and other conditions with just a few drops of blood. Holmes, a Stanford dropout, claimed her company did something no other scientist had done before: it used a proprietary blood-testing device.

Prosecutors maintained that the tests were manipulated, and that the executives lied about the capabilities of both the tests and the device, bilking investors out of nearly $1 billion.

Davila testified in court that Balwani's financial statements "weren't just projections, they were lies" and a "true flight from honest business practices."
