      Mars last night

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

At around 11 p.m., the Full Cold Moon will collide with the bright, reddish-colored planet Mars, which will be high overhead in our sky.

The Full Cold Moon is named after the cold temperatures we are experiencing in the Northern Hemisphere as winter approaches. The forecast for the Washington, D.C., area is for partly cloudy skies, which is good news for skywatchers.

Mars will be very close to the Moon in the sky during the Full Cold Moon. The Moon will actually pass in front of Mars for much of North America, but not the DMV, a phenomenon known as "occulting" the Red Planet. Mars will be less than a finger width away from the bottom of the Moon in our area, and will be closest at 10:43 p.m. EST. The view will be spectacular if you have binoculars or a small telescope with a wide-view eyepiece.

Several organizations will be livestreaming the occultation of Mars by the Full Cold Moon.




The Earth will also pass directly between Mars and the Sun on Wednesday at 11 p.m., marking the Red Planet's opposition. On December 1, the two planets were about 51 million miles apart. Mars will remain bright in our sky for the next few weeks before beginning to pull away from Earth and dim.

And don't miss NASA's coverage of Artemis I's splashdown off the coast of San Diego on Sunday at 11 a.m. EST. So far, the unmanned mission to the Moon has been picture-perfect in terms of the performance of the Orion spacecraft and the literally "out of this world" images it has taken.
