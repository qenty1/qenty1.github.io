      What is the light next to the moon tonight

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

This is shaping up to be a busy month for stargazers, as well as a busy night. We'll be able to see a very special eclipse on Wednesday (the evening of December 7 into the morning of December 8), the lunar occultation of Mars, and Mars will also be at opposition.

Mars is occulted by the moon when it passes between the Earth and Mars, obscuring our view of the Red Planet. According to Space.com, this will happen about an hour after sunset across North America on Wednesday night.

According to Sky Telescope, the moon will be in the constellation Taurus in the eastern sky, and you should be able to see Mars just to the left or lower left of it after sunset. Then, depending on your location, Mars will appear to travel behind the moon for a few minutes to an hour before emerging on the right side. It's also worth noting that the moon is full tonight, with the appropriately named Cold Moon putting on quite a show.

But that's not all that's going on in the sky tonight. Mars is also in opposition, which means the sun, Earth, and Mars form a perfect 180-degree line. This happens roughly every 26 months; the next opposition will take place in January 2025. Furthermore, Mars just passed perihelion, or its closest point to Earth in its orbit, a week ago, indicating that it is still relatively close by. (That is approximately 51 million miles away.)




Finally, Mars will be at its highest point in the night sky at opposition in 10 years, according to Astronomy Magazine, making for easy viewing through a telescope with no obstructions blocking your view near the horizon.

In a nutshell, there is no better time to purchase a telescope or binoculars to view Mars, but you must hurry to your nearest store to do so before the show. However, you will be able to see Mars with your own eyes.

And if the weather isn't favorable where you are, you can tune into live streams hosted by Los Angeles' Griffith Observatory or one jointly hosted by Texas' McDonald Observatory and Arizona's Lowell Observatory.
