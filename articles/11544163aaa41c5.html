      Star next to the moon

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

Each month, the Moon skims across the sky as it orbits Earth, passing by the planets. But every now and then, the Moon appears to pass in front of a planet from our perspective. An occultation occurs when the bright, ruddy Mars is temporarily obscured from view by Earth's lone natural satellite, and one will occur Wednesday night.

The Red Planet will be in opposition early Thursday morning, a date that many observers have already marked on their calendars. However, just a few hours earlier, late Wednesday night, the nearly Full Moon will occult Mars for most of the United States' skywatchers. (The only exceptions are those on the East and Gulf coasts, which will experience a close call.) Observers in Canada, Greenland, Iceland, the United Kingdom, northern Africa, and northwestern Europe can also see the event.

On December 7, an hour after sunset in the Midwest, Mars and the Moon are already more than 10 degrees high in the east and less than 2 degrees apart, with Mars to the lower left (east) of the Moon. They will continue to rise higher above the eastern horizon over the next few hours, nestled among the stars of Taurus the Bull. The pair is roughly halfway between the bright star Aldebaran (magnitude 0.9), which serves as the Bull's eye, and Elnath (magnitude 1.7), which marks the western horn of Taurus. Although the Full Moon does not officially occur until 11:08 p.m. EST, the face of our satellite will be fully illuminated to the naked eye.




Because the celestial geometry must be just right for the Moon to appear to cross in front of Mars, occultations occur only over a small portion of the globe. It's similar to an eclipse, which also follows a specific path across the globe. Even if you're not in the path, it's worth standing outside under the stars to admire our Moon as it passes close to Mars. Again, a telescope or binoculars will provide the best view, but the bright planet should be visible without any optical aid.

The Full Cold Moon, the year's final Full Moon, will occur shortly before midnight EST. And Mars will officially reach opposition shortly after midnight EST. Mars appears 17" wide through a telescope, large enough for larger scopes to reveal some of the planet's most prominent geological features. Mare Cimmerium and Mare Elysium should appear near the planet's center around midnight.
