      What planet is by the moon tonight

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

Wednesday will be a "magical" night for stargazing, with four celestial events taking place in the skies.

The day will conclude with the year's earliest sunset, Mars' opposition, and a rare occurrence in which the December full moon, known as the "cold moon," will temporarily eclipse Mars.

The sun will set just before 4:45 p.m. EST on Wednesday, with twilight ending at 5:49 p.m. EST. While it will bring about early darkness, it will also pave the way for a spectacular night of stargazing. Along with the full moon, viewers will be able to see Jupiter, Saturn, and Mars, the latter of which will be directly opposite the sun, a phenomenon known as Mars opposition.

According to NASA, the full moon on Wednesday is known as the cold moon because of the "long, cold nights" that winter brings, citing the Maine Farmers' Almanac. In the almanac, it's also known as the Frost Moon and Winter Moon, as well as the Yule Moon and Oak Moon in Europe.

However, the main event isn't just the moon. NASA stated that as Mars makes its closest and brightest appearance of the year, the moon will gradually move closer to the red planet. The moon will appear to eclipse Mars from view in some parts of the world, an event known as a lunaroccultation.

"Dec. 7 brings one of those magical moments when the sky changes dramatically before your very eyes," NASA says in a video highlighting skywatching highlights.




According to NASA, parts of North America, Europe, and Northern Africa will be able to see the occultation, while the southeast and east coasts of the United States will see the moon "just graze past Mars."

Most people should be able to observe the occultation for about an hour. According to NASA, those in Minneapolis will see Mars disappear around 9:03 p.m. CST and reappear around 10:11 p.m., while those in Los Angeles will see the phenomenon from around 6:31 p.m. PST to 7:31 p.m. The full moon will rise at 11:08 p.m. EST.

According to NASA, this will be a "relatively rare opportunity to observe a bright planet being occulted by the moon."

"Several times a year, the moon passes in front of planets in the night sky. In fact, it occults Mars at least a couple of times per year on average "NASA stated. "However, because each occultation is visible from only a small portion of the Earth's surface, it's uncommon for any particular location on Earth to see them frequently."

For those who are outside the regions where viewing the occultation is possible, or who may not have clear skies during the celestial events, there are live streams available to watch it unfold in real time.
