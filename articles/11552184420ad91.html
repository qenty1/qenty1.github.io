      Is mars visible right now

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

This week, Mars and the moon will put on a December celestial show that almost everyone in the United States can see.

The last full moon of the year, known as the cold moon or the "long night moon," occurs on Wednesday night.

The moon will be at its fullest just after 11 p.m. EST, and you'll have a little more time to admire it now that daylight saving time has ended.

According to NASA astronomer Michelle Thaller, Wednesday's event is a double treat for sky watchers because the full moon occurs when Mars is in opposition to the Earth and Sun.

"Not only do we have a full cold moon, but it's going to pass directly in front of Mars, so that's a wonderful thing called an occultation that will happen at about 10 p.m. Eastern Time," Thaller explained.

According to NASA, the moon passes in front of planets several times a year, but only a small portion of Earth is usually visible. The lunar occultation is rarely visible over such a wide area of the globe all at once. The majority of North America, Europe, and North Africa will be in attendance for this event.

The North American show will begin shortly after sunset, as the moon moves in front of Mars. Depending on where you look, the red planet will vanish behind the moon between 6:30 and 9:00 p.m. EST.




Unfortunately, the Southeast and the majority of the East Coast will miss out because Mars will be hidden behind the moon. According to NASA, viewers on the East Coast will see the moon pass close to Mars but not directly in front of it.

A clear sky is essential for those who do get to see lunar occultation. Some areas in the West, including Phoenix and San Francisco, will have mostly clear skies Wednesday night.

Mars disappears behind the moon at 9:03 p.m. CST and reappears at 10:11 p.m. CST in Minneapolis. The lunar occultation will be visible in Los Angeles at 6:31 p.m. PST.

"We often get very clear conditions with the winter sky, and the moonlight comes on through," Thaller said.

Mars will be in opposition to Earth on Thursday. Mars and the sun are on opposite sides of Earth during opposition, bringing the two planets closest together in their orbits. When it comes to the vastness of space, however, close is all relative. At its closest approach, Mars will still be 38.6 million miles away.

This will be the best view of Mars in two years, since the last opposition occurred in October 2020.
