      Kroger ceo woke

Published on Dec 11, 2022 by Admin.

Arkansas Senator Tom Cotton has been praised for mocking Kroger CEO Rodney McMullen's efforts to secure Republican support for his grocery merger plans.

Conservatives applaud Senator Cotton's response to Rodney McMullen's efforts to rally Republican support for his grocery merger plans.

Politicians examined the competitive impact of the proposed Kroger-Albertsons merger at a Senate Judiciary Committee hearing on November 29, 2022. The $25 billion deal would propel the companies to the dominance of Amazon and Walmart, raising concerns about inflated prices and the exclusion of small businesses.

Republican Senator Cotton chastised the businessman for seeking GOP assistance in opposing the Democratic Party's plans to block his company's merger and regulate it in the aftermath of the religious discrimination lawsuit in October.

Kroger was sued for $180,000 after two Arkansas employees were fired in 2019 for refusing to wear aprons with a rainbow heart embroidered on them.

According to the lawsuit, the workers believed the logo supported the LGBTQ community, which contradicted their "religious belief that homosexuality is a sin."

Although Kroger denied that the rainbow symbol indicated support for LGBTQ rights, the company has agreed to develop a religious accommodation policy. As part of the settlement, the supermarket chain will also provide more intensive religious discrimination training for managers, according to the Washington Post.

"Thank you Senator Cotton for addressing the elephant in the room," a supporter tweeted. "I worked for a major wireless company that relied on Republicans for a massive merger, only to become one of America's most woke companies."




A second person called out: "It's encouraging to see our Republicans finally removing the white gloves and putting on the boxing gloves. It's long overdue."

Watch the full interview between McMullen and Cotton here.

On October 14, Kroger announced the proposed plans, citing a desire to "unite around their purpose to feed the human spirit; deliver quality, value, convenience, and choice for customers; and increase associate wages and benefits."

The combined company would own nearly 5,000 stores in the United States, rivaling Walmart's 5,320. Albertsons and Kroger currently employ over 700,000 people across their portfolios, but senators are concerned that their joint venture will result in layoffs and price increases.

Democrats Jerry Nadler of New York, Pramila Jayapal of Washington, David Cicilline of Rhode Island, and Adam Smith of Washington all emphasized the negative effects of Kroger-Albertsons' control of roughly 13% of the US grocery market. "This acquisition threatens to create competition-stifling concentration in markets across the country, harming consumers, workers, and small businesses," they wrote to FTC Chair Lisa Khan.

"Corporate consolidation in the grocery retail industry results in higher prices and less consumer choice," they added. Meanwhile, complete market dominance equals less competition, so the acquisition may jeopardize stable wages and safe working conditions.
